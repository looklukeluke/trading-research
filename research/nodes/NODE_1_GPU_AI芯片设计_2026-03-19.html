★ 元思考: 这份报告的核心发现 Metacognitive Summary

🔎 核心分析焦点

GPU/AI芯片节点的真正问题不是"有没有需求"——三位认知代理人（Jensen/Lisa Su/Jim Keller）在需求方向上完全共识：AI计算需求是真实的、指数增长的。$1T订单可见性、$602B Hyperscaler CapEx、三个Scaling Law同时发力。

真正的分析焦点是：CUDA护城河能维持多久？GPU 70%+毛利率是可持续的还是历史异常？
Jensen说"CUDA是平台不只是芯片"，Lisa Su说"开放生态将胜出"，Jim Keller说"CUDA=利润堆叠"。三人对同一个市场给出了结构性不同的利润预期。这个分歧直接决定你应该买NVDA（Jensen对）、等AMD（Su对）、还是买AVGO避险（Keller对）。

✓ 三角验证共识

AI算力需求指数增长。$1T订单已签。推理成为新增长引擎。ASIC加速但补充而非替代GPU。供不应求至少持续到2027年。

✗ 三角验证分歧

CUDA护城河持久性。GPU定价权可持续性。2028年市场结构（80%垄断 vs 碎片化）。利润率是分析焦点，不是需求。

📈 交易指引

NVDA: 最确定但已大幅上涨，月线看是否做二浪横盘。Forward P/E ~25x。AMD: 暴跌55%后反弹潜力，MI400是催化剂。AVGO: ASIC设计垄断，"谁赢都受益"的避险选项。

⚠ 认知陷阱预警

卖方偏差: Jensen的$3-4T数字完美服务于"永远需要更多GPU"。幸存者偏差: CUDA成功后所有大胆押注都被框架为"远见"。Phase 0误差: 我们系统性低估了CapEx规模和ASIC加速度。

📖 三位代理人一句话摘要

Jensen Huang (GIANT, 8/10): "计算需求指数增长，agentic AI拐点已到。" — 27个预测89%准确，但作为最大受益方有系统性乐观偏差。$1T订单是事实，$3-4T预测需打折。

Lisa Su (INVESTOR, 7/10): "2026是AMD AI的inflection year。" — Zen押注($2→$200B)验证了战略判断力，但MI300X仅5-8%份额，"开放生态胜出"仍是信念而非事实。

Jim Keller (CONTRARIAN, 8/10): "NVIDIA做什么，我们就做相反的。" — 每个芯片架构都成功了(A4/Zen/HW3)，但Tenstorrent首次面临从0到规模化的商业考验。RISC-V 25%份额主要在嵌入式。

§0 Phase 0: 独立思考Independent Thinking (Before Search)

⚠ 诚实标注 Honesty Disclosure
本Phase 0标题为"搜索前独立思考"，但实际撰写时已读完三位代理人的完整MD文件（共48KB）。这是事后重构，不是真正的搜索前独立思考。其价值在于：明确标记预判偏差方向（如Phase 0低估了CapEx $700B和ASIC加速度44.6%），而非假装"猜对了"。详见28轮元思考 Phase V #12。

1. 节点解决什么问题？

GPU/AI芯片解决"计算供给"问题——将能源转化为智能。如果NVIDIA GPU明天消失：所有AI训练停止，推理退化到CPU速度（慢1000倍），$700B/年的AI基础设施投资变成废铁。GPU不是"加速器"，而是AI的"基础设施层"——就像电力对工业革命的角色。

2. 供给约束？

  • TSMC先进封装（CoWoS4）产能：需求100万片/月，实际产能~8万片
  • HBM分配：每GPU配套HBM翻倍增长（H100:80GB → B200:192GB → Rubin:288-384GB）
  • CUDA2生态护城河：6M+开发者、20年投资、cuDNN/TensorRT/Triton/NIM技术栈
  • 年度cadence制造压力：每年一代新架构（行业惯例2-3年）= 持续性供应紧张

3. 需求驱动力？

  • 三个Scaling Law1同时指数增长：预训练+后训练+推理时计算
  • Hyperscaler CapEx: $602B (+36% YoY)，75%用于AI
  • Agentic AI拐点：从"人用AI工具"到"AI agent自主执行"
  • 主权AI：各国政府建设本土AI算力
  • 推理需求崛起：DeepSeek R1证明"思考时间×100=更好答案"

4. 当前供需状态？

严重供不应求。$1T订单可见性3（5个月内从$500B翻倍），所有Blackwell/Rubin系统预售至2027年，NVIDIA FY2026数据中心收入$197.3B（+78% YoY）。

5. 未来2-3年变化方向？

  • 年度cadence持续：Rubin → Feynman(2028) → 下一代
  • 推理超过训练成为收入主引擎
  • ASIC替代加速：Google TPU v6、AWS Trainium2，ASIC增速44.6% vs GPU 16.1%
  • AMD MI400/500缩小性能差距，ROCm 7.0缩小软件差距
  • RISC-V从底层侵蚀ARM/x86架构基础
  • 如果AI CapEx放缓 → GPU高beta属性放大下行

§2 Phase 1: 认知代理人Cognitive Agents

GIANT

Jensen Huang — NVIDIA CEO & Co-Founder

选择理由: Jensen Huang是GPU→AI计算转型的定义者。NVIDIA在AI训练/推理市场占据约80%+份额，CUDA生态构成深厚护城河。他提出的三个Scaling Law框架定义了整个AI计算需求曲线。FY2026营收$215.9B、净利润$120B。27个预测追踪，89%准确率。可信度: 8/10。

行动证据（重于言论）

  1. CUDA 20年持续投入（2006-2026）: 在无人看好时投入数十亿开发GPU通用计算平台。2012年深度学习爆发才验证。最重要的战略赌注。
  2. 年度cadence制度化: Hopper → Blackwell → Rubin → Feynman，每年一代，打破行业2-3年周期。资本密集型，迫使竞争对手持续追赶。
  3. $20B收购Groq资产（2025-12）: 整合低延迟推理能力。防御性收购——Groq的推理速度曾被视为GPU替代方案。
  4. $1T订单锁定: 从$500B到$1T，5个月翻倍。不是预测而是已签合同的可见性。
  5. Vera Rubin NVL1445（GTC 2026）: 7种芯片集成为5个机架级计算机，3.6 EFLOPS FP4（Blackwell 3.3倍）。系统级交付，不只是芯片。
  6. $100B OpenAI合作备忘录: NVIDIA投资OpenAI，OpenAI在新数据中心使用NVIDIA系统。锁定最大AI客户。

核心判断

"Computing demand is growing exponentially — the agentic AI inflection point has arrived"计算需求指数增长——Agentic AI拐点已到来
  • AI基础设施投资将达$3-4T/年（2029-2030）
  • 推理是下一个增长引擎（第三个Scaling Law）
  • 10年后NVIDIA 75K员工 + 7.5M AI agent（100:1）
市场份额与估值详情

市场份额: AI训练GPU 80%+，AI推理GPU逐步增长。前4大客户占收入60%+。

估值: NVDA FY2026 $215.9B收入，$120B净利润。TTM市值~$3.2T。Forward P/E ~25x。

INVESTOR

Lisa Su — AMD Chair & CEO

选择理由: AMD是唯一真正的NVIDIA商用GPU竞争者。MI300X7/MI400是CUDA护城河的实时压力测试。如果AMD份额从5-8%升至15%+，意味着CUDA锁定正在松动。Su的判断经过10年验证（$2→$200B+）。可信度: 7/10。

行动证据

  1. Zen架构豪赌（2014-2017）: AMD濒临破产时全押新架构，服务器份额从2%升至30%+。
  2. MI300X拿下Azure+Meta: Microsoft EVP称"most cost-effective GPU"。Meta 100% Llama 405B在MI300X上推理。CUDA护城河裂缝。
  3. 收购狂潮补全栈: Xilinx($49B) + ZT Systems + Silo AI($665M) + Untether AI + Brium + Enosemi。2年5+次收购。
  4. ROCm 7.06发布: 原生MI355X支持，FP4/FP8低精度，推理3.5x提升。PyTorch一级支持。缩小CUDA差距到10-30%。
  5. MI400全系列+Helios（CES 2026）: MI455X 72GPU/机架，31TB HBM4，1.4PB/s。MI500(2027)预计1000x MI300X性能。
  6. Oracle大规模采购: 50,000颗MI450超级集群承诺。

核心判断

  • AI数据中心TAM $1T by 2030（与Jensen同方向但更聚焦芯片本身）
  • 整体CAGR 35%+，AI业务CAGR 80%+
  • 2026是AMD AI的"inflection year"
  • 开放生态（ROCm）对抗CUDA锁定——市场足够大时客户会寻求第二供应商
市场份额与估值详情

市场份额: AI GPU ~5-8%（2025），目标10-15%（2026）。分析师估NVIDIA峰值87%→2026年降至75%。MI300X出货327,000颗。

估值: AMD FY2025 $34.6B（+34% YoY），Q4 $10.3B。股价暴跌17%因保守指引——市场信心裂痕。

CONTRARIAN

Jim Keller — Tenstorrent CEO

选择理由: 唯一在Apple(A4/A5)、AMD(Zen)、Tesla(HW3)、Intel、Tenstorrent都参与过芯片架构设计的人。核心论点：NVIDIA的封闭生态不是唯一答案，RISC-V8+开源+小芯片可以从根本改变游戏规则。可信度: 8/10（技术9/10，商业6/10）。

行动证据

  1. A4/A5设计（Apple 2008-2012）: 奠定Apple Silicon基础。A4驱动初代iPad。
  2. Zen架构（AMD 2012-2015）: 从Bulldozer废墟中重新设计。AMD市值$20B→$200B+。
  3. Tesla HW3（2016-2018）: 证明非半导体公司可以从零自研芯片。面试时承诺"比竞品快10倍"。
  4. Ascalon9 RISC-V CPU（2025-10）: 22 SPECint2006/GHz，匹配ARM Neoverse V3/AMD Zen5。首个对标顶级的RISC-V实现。
  5. Tenstorrent $693M+$800M融资: 估值$3.2B。投资者包括Jeff Bezos、Samsung、Fidelity。
  6. CES 2026消费级AI加速器: 与Razer合作，Thunderbolt 5外接设备，Wormhole芯片。
  7. 中国+日本布局: Rapidus 2nm合作，前Arm China CEO合作推RISC-V进中国。

核心判断

"Whatever NVIDIA does, we'll do the opposite""NVIDIA做什么，我们就做相反的"——GDDR6 vs HBM, Ethernet vs NVLink, 开源 vs 闭源
  • "$7T→$1T: eliminate the margin stacking"——行业每层利润堆叠使成本远超必要
  • RISC-V是半导体的"Linux时刻"——专有架构在AI时代会被开源替代
  • IP授权 > 自产芯片（ARM模式但用RISC-V消除授权费）
对Node 1的结构性威胁
如果开源芯片+GDDR6+Ethernet能达到NVIDIA 70%性能而只需20%成本，GPU利润率将从70%+压缩到40-50%。
反驳与局限性

Tenstorrent仍然是前收入规模（$150M合同 vs NVIDIA $215B收入）。CUDA 6M+开发者生态不能通过fiat复制。Keller擅长"从0到1"但首次经历完整商业化。RISC-V 25%份额主要在嵌入式/IoT，数据中心GPU还未验证。

KNOW 三个Scaling Law ▾

The three scaling laws explained: Jensen defines AI compute demand as driven by three independent scaling curves, each growing exponentially. ① Pre-training scaling: more data + larger models + more compute = more capable base models. GPT-4 training used ~25,000 A100 GPUs for 90-100 days. GPT-5 reportedly used 5x that compute budget. Each generation roughly 10x the previous. ② Post-training scaling: synthetic data generation + RLHF + alignment tuning + distillation. This phase now consumes more compute than pre-training for frontier models. ③ Inference-time scaling: models like o1/o3 and DeepSeek R1 use 10-100x more compute per query by “thinking longer.” A simple query might use 1 unit; a complex reasoning query uses 100 units of the same hardware.

Why they’re multiplicative, not additive: The critical insight is that these three laws compound. A 10x increase in pre-training scale × 10x in post-training × 10x in inference-time compute = 1,000x total compute demand. This is why Jensen says “accelerated re-acceleration” — even if any single scaling law slows down, the other two can drive continued exponential growth in GPU demand. The compute demand is not a single S-curve that will plateau; it is three overlapping S-curves each at different maturity stages.

Counter-argument: The scaling laws assume that more compute always yields proportionally better results. There is growing evidence of diminishing returns in pre-training (GPT-5’s improvement over GPT-4 may be smaller than GPT-4 over GPT-3). If pre-training “hits a wall,” the entire demand model shifts to inference-time compute, which changes the hardware requirements (more inference chips, fewer training clusters). Jensen’s framing conveniently ignores this possibility because it would reduce GPU demand growth. (Cross-ref: m3 for seller bias on this framing.)

Strategic implication: If all three laws hold, GPU demand grows faster than supply for the next 3-5 years, favoring NVDA pricing power. If pre-training saturates but inference scaling continues, the market shifts toward inference-optimized chips — which favors AMD’s MI-series and custom ASICs over NVIDIA’s training-optimized H/B-series. The scaling law debate is not academic; it directly determines which companies benefit most.

KNOW CUDA生态 ▾

Ecosystem dimensions: CUDA began in 2006 — 20 years of continuous investment. The moat is not just CUDA the programming language; it is an entire multi-layered software stack. 6M+ developers worldwide. 800+ GPU-accelerated libraries covering every domain from molecular dynamics to financial modeling. 300+ SDKs/tools in the NGC catalog. 4,000+ startups building directly on CUDA. The ecosystem generates its own gravity — every new library makes CUDA harder to leave.

The layer-by-layer lock-in: cuDNN (neural network primitives — the foundation), TensorRT (inference optimization — production deployment), Triton (OpenAI’s attempt to create a CUDA alternative, but ironically still runs fastest on NVIDIA hardware), NCCL (multi-GPU communication — critical for training at 10,000+ GPU scale), NIM (model deployment microservices — the newest lock-in layer), and Dynamo (JIT compiler for PyTorch 2.0). Each layer adds incremental lock-in. A developer can replace any single layer, but replacing the entire stack simultaneously is prohibitively expensive.

AMD’s ROCm coverage: ROCm covers roughly 60% of this stack — the core compute primitives, basic PyTorch support, and HIP (CUDA translation layer). The remaining 40% is where developers get stuck: NIM has no ROCm equivalent, Dynamo’s ROCm backend is less optimized, and the 800+ third-party libraries overwhelmingly target CUDA first (many never port to ROCm). This is why the “10-30% performance gap” (see k6) understates the real competitive barrier — it measures raw compute, not deployment friction.

Erosion vectors: Despite the moat’s depth, three forces are slowly eroding it: (1) PyTorch 2.0’s torch.compile abstracts away the backend, making hardware-agnostic code the default; (2) Triton enables GPU kernel writing without CUDA knowledge; (3) JAX/XLA provides a completely non-CUDA path that Google uses internally. The moat is real today but has a 3-5 year half-life if these trends continue. (Cross-ref: m7 scenario analysis for what CUDA erosion means for stock prices.)

KNOW CoWoS封装瓶颈 ▾

What is CoWoS: Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate is TSMC’s advanced packaging technology that places multiple chiplets on a silicon interposer, enabling the massive dies that AI accelerators require. Every major AI chip (NVIDIA H100/B100/B200, AMD MI300X, Google TPU v5/v6) uses CoWoS or a comparable advanced packaging approach. It is the shared physical bottleneck for the entire AI chip industry — not a design limitation but a manufacturing capacity constraint.

Capacity timeline: TSMC CoWoS capacity was ~80K wafers/month in early 2025. Target: 200K/month by end of 2026, 350K/month by end of 2027. Expansion requires new fabs and specialized tooling (mainly from ASM International for hybrid bonding equipment and Besi for die bonding). Even with this aggressive expansion, demand forecasts suggest 500K+/month needed by 2027. The gap persists — supply cannot catch demand within the planning horizon.

Why NVIDIA’s annual cadence makes this worse: Each new NVIDIA architecture requires more advanced packaging, not less. Blackwell B200: 2 GPU dies + 1 Grace CPU on CoWoS. Vera Rubin: even larger interposer for NVL144 configuration. The annual cadence creates structural shortage because TSMC must qualify new packaging configurations every 12 months while simultaneously ramping volume on the previous generation. Samsung and Intel Foundry are building competing advanced packaging, but customer qualification takes 12-18 months — no near-term relief.

Investment implication: CoWoS bottleneck means that even if demand exceeds $1T (see k3), NVIDIA cannot ship faster than TSMC can package. This is paradoxically bullish for NVIDIA pricing power (scarcity maintains ASP premiums) but bearish for revenue growth rate (capacity-capped). It is also bullish for TSMC (guaranteed utilization, pricing power on packaging services) and for ASM International/Besi (the equipment makers are the true bottleneck behind the bottleneck).

Architecture details: Vera Rubin was announced at GTC 2026 as NVIDIA’s next-generation AI platform. It integrates 7 different chip types: Rubin GPU (TSMC N3 process, successor to Blackwell’s N4), Vera CPU (ARM-based, replacing the x86-compatible Grace), CX9 DPU (data processing unit), ConnectX-8 NIC (800Gb/s networking), NVLink switches, retimers, and power management ICs. The Kyber prototype showed 144 GPUs in vertical orientation for improved cooling and density. This is not just a bigger GPU — it is a system architecture shift where NVIDIA sells entire rack-scale computers, not individual chips. The ASP per system is estimated at $5-10M, transforming NVIDIA from a component vendor into a systems integrator.

Critical architectural signal: Vera CPU uses ARM cores, not x86. This means NVIDIA is betting AGAINST x86 for AI data centers. Grace (the current CPU) was already ARM-based, but Vera doubles down by making ARM the default CPU architecture for all future NVIDIA AI platforms. This has enormous implications: it validates ARM’s push into data center compute, threatens Intel/AMD’s x86 server CPU business, and aligns NVIDIA with the mobile/embedded ecosystem where ARM dominates. For AMD specifically, losing the CPU socket in NVIDIA AI systems means losing ~$2,000-5,000 per server in EPYC revenue.

Key features: native support for MI350/MI355X architecture, FP4/FP8 quantization (matching CUDA’s latest precision formats), native PyTorch compile backend (torch.compile works out-of-box), and HIP (CUDA translation layer) now covering ~95% of CUDA API surface area. Real-world benchmarks: MI300X running ROCm 7.0 on Llama 405B inference achieves ~85% of H100 throughput at a lower price/performance ratio. For training at scale (1,000+ GPUs), the gap is larger: ~75% of NVIDIA throughput due to RCCL (ROCm’s multi-GPU communication library) being less optimized than NCCL at 10,000+ GPU scale. For single-GPU inference workloads, ROCm is competitive; for massive distributed training, NVIDIA retains a meaningful advantage.

Where the real gap lives: The 10-30% headline number measures raw compute performance. But the deployment gap is wider: CUDA has TensorRT for production inference optimization (no ROCm equivalent of comparable maturity), NCCL for multi-GPU training at extreme scale (RCCL is functional but less battle-tested beyond 4,000 GPUs), and 10x more third-party library support. A researcher can prototype on ROCm; a production ML engineer deploying at scale faces friction that doesn’t show up in benchmarks. (Cross-ref: k2 for the full CUDA ecosystem lock-in analysis.)

If this trajectory continues, ROCm 8.0 (expected with MI400 Helios in 2026) could close the gap to <10% on most workloads. The question is not “is ROCm good enough today?” but “will ROCm be good enough by 2027-2028 to be the default second-source?” Microsoft and Meta are betting yes. KNOW RISC-V ▾

Current market position: RISC-V is an open-source instruction set architecture (ISA) with zero licensing fees. As of December 2025, it holds 25% global market share — but this is almost entirely in embedded/IoT applications (microcontrollers, automotive MCUs, consumer electronics, wearables). Data center penetration is <1%. The 25% number is impressive for adoption speed but misleading about data center relevance — it is like saying “Linux has 90% server share” when most of that share took 20 years to build.

The path to data center: Keller’s thesis requires RISC-V to traverse three stages: ① Edge AI inference (Tenstorrent’s current target — low-power AI chips for robotics and automotive, shipping now in small volumes); ② Specialized accelerator cores (like Google TPU’s scalar processing units, which could theoretically be RISC-V instead of proprietary — no public deployment yet); ③ General-purpose data center compute (requires full OS ecosystem support: Linux runs on RISC-V but major ISV support from VMware, Oracle DB, SAP, etc. is years away). Each stage is a necessary prerequisite for the next.

Key risk — fragmentation: Unlike ARM (where ARM Holdings controls the ISA and ensures compatibility), RISC-V has multiple competing custom extensions. Different vendors add different instructions for vector processing, crypto, AI acceleration. This fragmentation could slow enterprise adoption because software compiled for one RISC-V implementation may not run optimally on another. Keller’s counter-argument: “That’s exactly what they said about Linux vs Unix in 1998.” Linux won despite fragmentation concerns because the economic incentive (free vs $50K/server) was overwhelming. RISC-V’s economic incentive is similar: free ISA vs ARM’s 1-2% royalty.

Investment implication: RISC-V is a 5-10 year thesis, not a 2026 trade. For trend traders (see m8), there is no liquid pure-play RISC-V stock to trade today. Tenstorrent is private ($3.2B valuation, last round Nov 2025). The indirect plays are: shorting ARM Holdings if RISC-V adoption accelerates (ARM’s royalty model is the most directly threatened), or going long Tenstorrent investors (Samsung, Hyundai, LG) as a proxy. But the honest assessment: this is too early-stage for a trend trading strategy. File it as a structural thesis to re-evaluate in 2028. KNOW Ascalon RISC-V CPU ▾

Benchmark breakthrough: Tenstorrent announced Ascalon in October 2025. The top-end Ascalon-X variant: 8-wide out-of-order execution, achieves 22 SPECint2006/GHz at 2.5GHz+ on Samsung SF4X (4nm process). This is the first RISC-V CPU core to reach performance parity with top-tier commercial CPU IP. Comparison: ARM Neoverse V3 ~22 SPECint2006/GHz; AMD Zen 5 ~23 SPECint2006/GHz; Intel Granite Rapids ~21 SPECint2006/GHz. Ascalon-X matches ARM and is within 5% of AMD’s best.

IP licensing economics: ARM charges 1-2% royalty on chip ASP + upfront license fee ($10M+). RISC-V ISA itself is free. Tenstorrent charges for Ascalon IP implementation (estimated $5-20M license + modest royalty, significantly below ARM’s rates). For a hyperscaler building 1M chips/year at $500 ASP: ARM royalties = $5-10M/year; Tenstorrent = potentially $2-5M/year or less. At scale, the savings compound — and for a company like Google or Amazon building 10M+ chips/year across multiple product lines, the difference becomes $50-100M annually.

Product line strategy: Ascalon comes in multiple variants: Ascalon-X (high-performance, server-class), Ascalon-H (high-efficiency, mid-range), Ascalon-M (mainstream), and Ascalon-S (small, embedded). This mirrors ARM’s Neoverse/Cortex strategy of covering the full spectrum from data center to IoT. The difference: Tenstorrent’s business model is IP licensing (like ARM) + reference SoC designs (unlike ARM). Keller is building a company that could replace ARM’s position in the ecosystem while using an open-source ISA that no single company controls.

Credibility assessment: The SPECint numbers are impressive, but SPECint2006 is a single-threaded integer benchmark — it does not measure memory bandwidth, multi-threaded scaling, cache hierarchy efficiency, or real-world server workload performance. Ascalon-X matching Neoverse V3 on SPECint does NOT mean it matches Neoverse V3 on SPEC CPU 2017 or on actual cloud workloads. The gap between “competitive benchmark” and “production-ready server CPU” is 2-3 years of validation, software optimization, and customer qualification. Keller’s track record (Zen, Apple A-series) suggests he can close this gap, but it is not yet closed. (Cross-ref: k8 for the broader RISC-V ecosystem context.)

KNOW ASIC加速趋势 ▾

The acceleration gap: ASIC (custom silicon) revenue is growing at 44.6% vs GPU revenue growth of 16.1%. This 2.8x growth rate differential is the most underappreciated structural shift in the AI chip market. It means that within 3-5 years, custom silicon could rival GPU revenue in total TAM — even as GPU revenue continues to grow in absolute terms.

Detailed roadmap per hyperscaler: Google: TPU v6 (“Trillium”) in production, 4.7x training improvement over v5. All Google AI services (Search, Gemini, Cloud TPU) run on TPUs. TPU v7 expected 2027. AWS: Trainium2 shipping in UltraClusters (65K chips per cluster), 4x training improvement over Trainium1. Trainium3 on TSMC 3nm expected H2 2026. Meta: MTIA v2 for ranking/recommendation workloads, exploring v3 for training. Not yet competitive for LLM training but reduces Meta’s NVIDIA dependency for inference. Microsoft: Maia 100 deployed in Azure, focused on inference optimization for Azure OpenAI Service. Apple: Exploring Baltra server chip (Broadcom partnership, TSMC N3P) for Apple Intelligence workloads.

The critical nuance: Every $100B+ tech company is developing custom silicon. But ALL of them still buy NVIDIA GPUs too. Custom ASIC supplements, not fully replaces, GPU compute. The reason: ASICs are optimized for specific workloads (Google TPU for transformer training, AWS Trainium for specific model architectures), while GPUs handle the “long tail” of diverse AI workloads. The equilibrium is probably 40-50% custom ASIC + 50-60% GPU for large hyperscalers, and 90%+ GPU for everyone else (enterprises, startups, sovereign AI).

Broadcom’s hidden dominance: Broadcom captures ~80% of custom ASIC design services revenue. When Google, Amazon, or Apple want to build a custom chip, they hire Broadcom to design it. This makes AVGO the “arms dealer” play — it wins regardless of which hyperscaler’s custom chip succeeds. Broadcom’s AI revenue grew from $3.5B (FY2023) to $12.2B (FY2025), and CEO Hock Tan projects $60-90B in AI revenue by 2027. If the ASIC acceleration trend continues (see m2 for why Phase 0 underestimated this), Broadcom may be the biggest structural winner in the AI chip ecosystem after NVIDIA.
